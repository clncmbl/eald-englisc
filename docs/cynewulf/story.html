The story of Cynewulf and Cyneheard appears in the Anglo-Saxon Chronicles for the year 755. (At this point in the Chronical, however, the entries are from two to three years off. Specifically, the Chronicle introduces this error for entries that it says are for years 754 to 845. The correct range for these entries is from 756 to 848.) The Chronicle is traditionally thought to have been started during the reign of Alfred (871-899), which would mean that the story was passed down orally or in writing for at least a few generations before being written down in the Chronicle.

The story

Cynewulf and the West Saxon witan took from Sigebryht his rule, except over Hampshire, for unrighteous deeds. Sigebryht held Hampshire until he slew Cumbran, the ealdorman who had remained with him the longest. Cynewulf then drove Sigebryht away to the Andred forest where he remained until a swineherd stabbed him to death at the stream at Privett, thereby avenging ealdorman Cumbran.

As king, Cynewulf often fought in great battles against the Britons.

Thirty one years into Cynewulf's rule, he decided to drive out an atheling named Cyneheard, who was Sigebryht's brother. Cyneheard, however, learned that the king, with a small werod, went to visit a woman in Merton, and pursued him there.
